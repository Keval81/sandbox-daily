# The $2 Million Handshake: Iran's Real Endgame Was Never Nuclear

**The Islamabad talks didn't collapse. They were never going to close.**

Twenty-one hours. That's how long American and Iranian negotiators sat across a table in Pakistan before walking away from the highest-level direct engagement between Washington and Tehran in decades. No deal. No reopened strait. No assurances from Tehran that it would stop short of rebuilding nuclear weapons capabilities. JD Vance flew home with a set of red lines and a statement to read out. Iran's state media blamed "excessive" US demands and presumably went back to doing sums.

Here is the question nobody is asking loudly enough: what if Iran didn't need the talks to succeed?

---

## Twenty-One Hours in Islamabad

Two delegations sat down for long enough to produce a short-haul flight's worth of jet lag, exchanged positions that were apparently irreconcilable, and parted ways with a fragile ceasefire still technically in place but no structural agreement underneath it. Vance said the US had made its red lines clear. Tehran said Washington was being unreasonable. Both statements are probably true, and neither one explains why the talks were always going to end this way.

Australia's Foreign Minister Penny Wong called the outcome "disappointing." Which is the diplomatic equivalent of describing a house fire as a "heating incident." Wong also said the priority now must be to continue the ceasefire and return to negotiations — a sentence that contains within it a quiet admission that the ceasefire is, at this point, doing most of the structural work.

Treasurer Jim Chalmers was more candid about the economic reality: "The pressure on households won't just disappear the moment the ceasefire sticks or the day the Strait of Hormuz is properly opened." The word *properly* is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence. And that framing — whether Chalmers intended it or not — points directly at Iran's actual play.

---

## The Toll Booth Nobody Wanted to Name

Shadow Defence Minister James Paterson named it. He said: "It is critically important that the end of this conflict does not begin with a new toll booth for the IRGC."

*Toll booth for the IRGC.* Sit with that phrase for a moment.

Because Iran was reportedly considering charging fees of up to **US$2 million per ship** transiting the Strait of Hormuz — with Oman positioned as a prospective sharer of the proceeds. Two million dollars. Per ship. Through the single most critical oil chokepoint on the planet.

This isn't a negotiating position. This is a business model.

---

## Why the Strait Is Worth More Closed Than Open (For Iran)

Think about it this way. Imagine you live next to the only bridge connecting two major cities. You've spent years in conflict with the city on the other side, and both cities have been throwing rocks at each other. Now a ceasefire is being negotiated.

Here's your problem: once there's peace, you're just a guy who lives near a bridge.

But if you can establish — through precedent, through threat, through the simple fact of having controlled the chokepoint at a critical moment — that ships using your bridge pay you a fee? Now you're not just a guy near a bridge. You're an infrastructure company. With a standing army.

The strait hasn't been properly reopened. A fragile ceasefire exists. And into that fragile, ambiguous space, Tehran has reportedly floated the idea of transit fees. Two million dollars per vessel. Routed, in some configurations, through Oman.

The IRGC, which opponents of this arrangement have specifically named as the likely revenue recipient, would go from being a sanctioned paramilitary force to being, effectively, a toll authority. One with missiles and a history of using them, but still. There's something almost admirable about the audacity of the concept — in the same way that a man who just robbed a bank and is now offering to sell you parking validation is technically providing a service.

---

## What Iran Actually Walked Away With

Here's the part that should concentrate minds. Iran left Islamabad without agreeing to anything — no nuclear commitments, no reopening of the strait, nothing. But the ceasefire remains. And as long as the ceasefire remains, Tehran controls the timeline.

Every day the strait stays in its current state is a day the world's oil markets stay anxious. Every anxious oil market is a negotiating lever. And every negotiating session where Tehran refuses to budge on the transit fee question is another day that the concept of a $2 million toll becomes less outrageous and more, regrettably, structural.

This is how leverage compounds. You don't need to win the negotiation. You just need the negotiation to continue indefinitely while the facts on the ground shift in your favour.

Wong is right that escalation would impose further human and economic costs. Chalmers is right that even a reopened strait won't immediately ease household pressure — Australia and others are bracing for months of economic drag regardless of what happens at the next round of talks. These are sober, accurate assessments. They are also, from Tehran's perspective, useful information. If the world is going to absorb months of pain regardless of whether the strait opens tomorrow or in six months, then Iran loses very little by waiting.

---

## The Nuclear Question Was Always a Sideshow

Vance left Islamabad without assurances that Iran would not rebuild nuclear weapons capabilities. That's a significant failure by any conventional measure. But consider what the nuclear question is, in structural terms: it's a ceiling on Iran's ambitions, a constraint on what Tehran is permitted to become. The transit fee proposal is different in kind. It's not about weaponry at all. It's about revenue. It's about transforming a geopolitical crisis into a recurring income stream, denominated in dollars, with Oman as a co-investor.

Nuclear weapons are a deterrent. A $2 million per-ship toll on the Strait of Hormuz is a subscription service.

One of these makes Iran more dangerous. The other makes Iran, in a horrible and perverse way, more *normal* — a state that derives economic benefit from a geographic advantage it controls. The US and its allies have spent decades worrying about the former. Tehran may have spent the last several months noticing that nobody had a good answer to the latter.

---

## What Twenty-One Hours Actually Bought

The Islamabad talks broke down after 21 hours because the two sides couldn't agree on terms. That framing implies a deal was possible and simply wasn't reached. A sharper reading is that Iran has identified a position more durable than any deal — one that doesn't require American sign-off, doesn't require reopening the strait on anyone else's schedule, and converts the world's dependence on Persian Gulf oil into a line item on the IRGC's balance sheet.

James Paterson was right to name it. **A toll booth for the IRGC is not the end of the conflict. It is the institutionalisation of it.** And twenty-one hours in Islamabad was Iran signalling — politely, through an interpreter, after a very long day — that it already knows that.
