# The Protection Racket

## What Happened

The sequence is straightforward, and damning. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz publicly criticised America's approach to Iran, saying the Americans "clearly have no strategy" and suggesting they were being humiliated diplomatically. Donald Trump fired back on Truth Social, accusing Merz of not knowing what he was talking about and implying he was soft on Iran acquiring nuclear weapons. Shortly after this exchange, US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth ordered the withdrawal of American troops from Germany, to be carried out over six to twelve months.

Germany's Defence Minister Boris Pistorius called the withdrawal "foreseeable." NATO sought clarification on the move. And just like that, the largest US military presence in Europe began packing its bags.

The official American complaint about Germany, repeated for years, has been about money. Trump previously called Germany "delinquent" for failing to meet NATO's benchmark of 2% of GDP on military spending. He has repeatedly argued the US shoulders too much of the military burden while European countries benefit from security without paying enough. Fair enough — that's a legitimate gripe, and one that predates Trump by decades.

So Germany paid up.

## The Cheque That Bounced

Germany is now projected to spend €105.8 billion on defence in 2027. Its overall defence expenditure is set to reach 3.1% of GDP once other defence-linked spending is included. That's not just meeting the 2% NATO benchmark — it's blowing past it by more than half. Germany has significantly boosted military spending in recent years, going from the stingy freeloader of American complaints to one of the biggest defence spenders on the continent.

And the reward for this compliance? A troop withdrawal announced in the aftermath of a diplomatic spat.

This is the part where you're supposed to nod along and accept the framing that Europe still isn't doing enough, that the goalposts moved to 5% because the threat environment changed. After a NATO summit in The Hague, there were references to allied progress towards spending 5% of GDP on defence. So the target went from 2% — which Germany wasn't meeting — to 2% — which Germany exceeded — to 3% — which Germany is on track to beat — to 5%, a figure that would require most European nations to essentially become garrison states.

This is not a negotiation. This is a ratchet. And ratchets only turn one way.

## The Loudest Quiet Part

Here's what most commentary misses: the troop withdrawal didn't follow a spending dispute. It followed a **political disagreement**. Merz criticised American strategy on Iran. Trump took it personally. Troops were pulled.

The spending was never the real issue. It was the pretext. The actual demand isn't for euros — it's for silence.

Think about this the way you'd think about any other relationship where one party keeps changing the terms. Your landlord says the rent is too low, so you agree to pay more. Then you pay more, and they say actually the problem is that you complained about the plumbing. At some point you stop negotiating the rent and start asking what the landlord actually wants.

Trump wanted allied participation in operations to reopen the Strait of Hormuz after Iran reportedly restricted traffic through the strait. That demand, alongside US and Israeli strikes as part of a broader military escalation involving Iran, is the backdrop. Germany's sin wasn't insufficient spending. It was insufficient enthusiasm for someone else's war.

## What Withdrawal Actually Means

To understand why this matters beyond the symbolism, you need to understand what US forces in Germany actually do. Germany isn't just a place where American soldiers happen to be stationed, the way you might park your car in a friend's driveway. It's more like the entire engine of the car is in the driveway.

Germany hosts a major share of the US military presence in Europe. More importantly, it serves as a logistical hub, staging ground, and strategic base for US operations across Europe, the Middle East, and beyond. Imagine you run a shipping company. Your main warehouse isn't where your customers are — it's where all the routes intersect, where the sorting happens, where the trucks refuel. Now imagine shutting that warehouse down to punish one customer. You haven't just hurt them. You've kneecapped your own operation.

This is the peculiar genius of the move: it damages American strategic capability in order to send a message to an ally who had the temerity to voice a foreign policy opinion out loud. It's the geopolitical equivalent of keying your own car because your passenger complained about the music.

Every logistics chain, command structure, and intelligence-sharing arrangement that runs through German soil gets disrupted. The withdrawal doesn't just weaken Germany's sense of security — it degrades America's ability to project power across an entire hemisphere.

## The 5% Fantasy

The migration from 2% to the whispered 5% target deserves its own examination, because it reveals the mechanism perfectly.

At 2%, American officials could point to European freeloading with some justification. Most NATO allies were genuinely underspending, and Germany was one of the worst offenders. But Germany responded. It passed massive defence spending increases. It is on track for 3.1% of GDP. By any reasonable standard, this is a country that heard the complaint and acted on it — dramatically.

So the benchmark moved. Five percent of GDP on defence would be, for most European economies, somewhere between crippling and fictional. It's not a serious policy target. It's a number chosen because it can't be met — which means the leverage never expires.

The moment you act like a partner rather than a client, the terms change.

## What Germany Actually Learned

Pistorius calling the withdrawal "foreseeable" is the most telling detail in this entire episode. Not shocking. Not outrageous. Foreseeable. The German defence establishment saw this coming, which means they've already absorbed the lesson: compliance doesn't buy security. Only obedience does. And obedience, unlike compliance, has no upper limit.

Germany now faces a choice that has nothing to do with percentages of GDP. It can continue trying to satisfy demands that will always escalate, or it can start building the kind of independent European defence capability that makes the question irrelevant.

The troop withdrawal, intended as punishment, may end up being the most clarifying gift Germany has received in decades. Nothing concentrates the mind like discovering that your bodyguard was only ever a landlord — and that the rent was always going to go up.
