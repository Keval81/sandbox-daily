# The Country That Forgot to Have Children

Britain is dying faster than it can replace itself, and the only thing keeping the lights on is the very thing half its politicians campaign against.

## The Numbers Nobody Wants to Read Aloud

Starting this year — 2026 — more people will die in the United Kingdom than will be born. The Office for National Statistics projects this will happen **every single year** from now on.

The country that once populated an empire now cannot populate itself.

Over the next decade, the ONS expects 6.4 million births and 6.9 million deaths. That's a deficit of nearly half a million people — a hole in the national fabric roughly the size of Manchester, simply vanishing. The UK population will still grow to around 71 million by 2034, and it will even keep climbing until a peak sometime in the 2050s. But every ounce of that growth comes from one source: immigration. Net migration is projected to add 2.2 million people between 2024 and 2034. Without it, Britain shrinks.

The previous assumption — the comfortable one — was that the UK population would keep growing until late in the century. That assumption is dead. The revised projection of a 2050s peak represents considerably slower growth than anyone in Whitehall had been planning for. Britain is not just ageing. It is ageing faster than expected, and its own statisticians have been forced to revise the timeline.

## The Dependency Equation Britain Refuses to Solve

Here is the part that should concentrate minds but instead gets buried under culture-war noise: by 2034, pensioners will make up around one fifth of the population. The pensioner population is expected to grow by 1.8 million. The number of children, meanwhile, is projected to **fall** by 1.6 million.

The working-age population — the people who actually fund the pensions, staff the hospitals, drive the buses, and pay the taxes — is expected to grow by just 1.5 million. And where does that growth come from? Not from a sudden outbreak of British fecundity. It comes from those 7.3 million long-term immigrants the ONS expects to arrive over the decade (offset by 5.1 million long-term emigrants heading the other way).

Think of the national economy as a restaurant. The dining room is filling up with pensioners ordering the prix fixe — pensions, NHS care, social services. The kitchen, where the working-age population cooks and serves, is barely growing. And the pipeline of new kitchen staff being trained — the children — is actively shrinking. The only reason the restaurant doesn't collapse tonight is that people keep walking in off the street and asking for a job. Those people are immigrants. The Home Office's stated position is that it wants to reduce migration while still attracting "the brightest and the best" — firing the line cooks and hoping celebrity chefs will wander in.

## Why This Isn't a Policy Problem — It's a Maths Problem

Most public debates about immigration operate as though Britain is making a generous offer. Come to our country, enjoy our services, share in our prosperity. The framing implies that immigration is a favour Britain extends, and one it can withdraw at will.

The ONS numbers demolish this framing so completely it's almost embarrassing.

Without net migration, Britain's population doesn't just plateau — it contracts. Fewer workers means a smaller tax base. A smaller tax base means less money for the NHS, for state pensions, for the military, for everything. More pensioners drawing from the pot and fewer workers filling it is not a political opinion. It is arithmetic.

Let's make this concrete with a thought experiment. Imagine the government got its wish tomorrow: net migration drops to zero. No more of those 2.2 million additional people over the decade. What happens?

The working-age population doesn't grow by 1.5 million. It shrinks. The pensioner population still grows by 1.8 million, because those people are already here and already ageing. The ratio of workers to retirees — the single most important number in any welfare state — craters. Every nurse, every care worker, every taxpayer is now carrying a heavier load.

And it gets grimmer. Experts have warned that end-of-life care services are not properly prepared for the coming increase in deaths. Too much end-of-life spending flows through emergency hospital care rather than planned systems. Britain is short of workers to care for its ageing population. It hasn't built the systems to handle the dying part efficiently, either. We are, to put it with characteristic British understatement, somewhat underprepared.

## The Spike That Wasn't a Baseline

One more twist in the data. The post-Brexit immigration surge was a temporary spike, not a long-term baseline — that's now the ONS's official position. This matters enormously, because it means the recent years of high net migration — the ones that generated all those tabloid front pages — were the exception, not the rule. Future projections assume migration will be lower.

So the people shouting loudest about reducing immigration may get their wish by default. And they will not enjoy the result. Because the demographic problem doesn't go away when migration slows. It gets worse. The surge was, in a sense, a lucky accident — a brief period when Britain received precisely the influx of working-age people it desperately needed. Treating it as a crisis rather than a lifeline is like complaining about the fire brigade showing up uninvited.

## The Sentence No Politician Will Say

Every major policy question in Britain — how to fund the NHS, how to pay for pensions, how to staff care homes, how to sustain public services — runs through the same bottleneck. There aren't enough young people. There aren't enough workers. There aren't enough taxpayers. And the only proven, immediate, available solution is the one that remains politically radioactive.

Britain didn't hold a referendum on whether to become a country with more coffins than cradles. Nobody voted for a demographic structure in which a fifth of the population is retired and the supply of children is falling by 1.6 million. It just happened, the way these things happen — quietly, over decades, one family decision at a time.

But the consequences are not quiet. They are loud, expensive, and coming fast. A country that cannot replace its own population has exactly two options: welcome the people who can keep it running, or watch the system buckle under the weight of its own age.

**Britain needs immigrants more than immigrants need Britain.** The sooner the political class speaks this sentence aloud — plainly, without embarrassment, without wrapping it in euphemisms about "the brightest and the best" — the sooner it can start building a country that actually works for the century ahead. Until then, it will keep doing what it does best: arguing about the lifeboat while the ship takes on water.
